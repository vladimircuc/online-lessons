Chapter 1 / 6 The Problem

Wait — Does Ford-Fulkerson Actually Work? 🤔

Last lesson you learned Ford-Fulkerson: find an augmenting path, push flow, repeat. But your notes ask a sharp question right at the top: "Is this algorithm well-designed?"

That means three things: does it always terminate? Does it always find the true maximum? Is it fast enough to be useful?

⚠️ Spoiler: it's not always well-designed. There's a real scenario where Ford-Fulkerson can run forever and never give you an answer.

The Termination Problem

If all edge capacities are integers, Ford-Fulkerson is fine. Each iteration adds at least 1 unit of flow, and flow is bounded by the total capacity, so it must stop eventually.

But what if capacities are irrational numbers — like √2 or π? Then each iteration might add a tiny, shrinking amount of flow. The algorithm can keep running, getting closer and closer to the max but never actually reaching it. Infinite loop. 💀

🔴 Ford-Fulkerson with DFS can loop forever on irrational capacities. This is a real, known theoretical weakness — not just a coding bug.

Concrete Example — The Golden Ratio Trap 🌀

The classic example uses φ = (1+√5)/2 ≈ 1.618, the golden ratio. It's irrational, and it has a special property: 1/φ = φ − 1 ≈ 0.618. Every power of 1/φ is also irrational.

Imagine a simple network: s → A → t, with one extra edge A → B and B → t. The edge s→A has capacity 1, edge A→B has capacity 1/φ ≈ 0.618, and the rest have large capacity. DFS unluckily keeps picking a path through A→B every other iteration:

But even with integers — how do we know FF finds the actual maximum and not just a local one? That requires a proof. And that proof needs a new concept: the cut.

Chapter 2 / 6 New Concept

A Cut — Slicing the Graph in Two ✂️

Forget flow for a second. Imagine the graph as a country, and you draw a border through it. On one side you have the source s, on the other side you have the sink t.

That border is called an s-t cut. Formally it splits all vertices V into two groups:

Contains the source s. All the vertices on s's side of the border.
Contains the sink t. All the vertices on t's side of the border.
📐 Rules: Every vertex goes in exactly one side. s must be in L. t must be in R. That's it — everything else can go anywhere.

Cut Capacity — The Cost of the Border

Once you draw the border, look at all the edges that cross from L to R (going left-to-right, s-side to t-side). Add up their capacities. That total is the capacity of the cut, written cap(L, R).

Important: edges going R→L (right to left, backwards across the border) do NOT count. Only edges going in the forward direction, L→R.

🤔 Why does cap(L,R) matter? Think about it physically: ALL flow from s to t must at some point cross the border from L to R. The border is a bottleneck for the entire network. The cut capacity is the absolute maximum any flow could ever be.

Chapter 3 / 6 The Upper Bound

Every Cut Is An Upper Bound on Flow 🚧

Here's a key insight before the big theorem. Think about the border analogy again:

ALL flow from s to t must cross the border from L to R at some point. There's no way around it — s is on one side, t is on the other, so every unit of flow has to cross.

That means: size(f) ≤ cap(L, R) — for ANY cut (L, R) you pick.

📦 Think of it like shipping packages. If there are only 3 roads leading out of the source's city, no matter how many warehouses and routes exist inside that city, the max packages you can ship is limited by those 3 outgoing roads.

Why Exactly? (The Proof Intuition)

Take any valid flow f and any cut (L, R). The flow going from L to R across the cut minus any flow going backwards (R to L) equals the total flow size.

size(f) = (flow on L→R edges) − (flow on R→L edges)

Since flow on R→L edges is ≥ 0, this means:

size(f) ≤ flow on L→R edges ≤ capacity of L→R edges = cap(L, R)

This works for every single cut you could possibly draw. Every cut gives you a valid upper bound. So if you find a cut with capacity 9, you know the max flow can never exceed 9.

💡 This is super useful! If you find a flow of size X AND a cut with capacity X, you've proven both are optimal simultaneously — the flow can't go higher (cut proves it) and the cut can't go lower (flow proves it).

The tightest upper bound comes from the minimum cut. All other cuts give looser, less useful bounds. So: max flow ≤ min cut capacity.
Chapter 4 / 6 The Big Result

The Max-Flow Min-Cut Theorem 🏆

We just showed max flow ≤ min cut. But the theorem says something stronger:

Max Flow = Min Cut
The maximum possible flow equals the capacity of the minimum cut. Exactly equal. Always.

This is one of the most beautiful results in computer science. Two completely different things — optimal flow through a network, and the weakest bottleneck in the network — turn out to be the same number.

Why Does This Prove Ford-Fulkerson is Correct?

Remember Ford-Fulkerson stops when there's no augmenting path from s to t in the residual graph. At that point, is it truly at maximum flow? Let's see:

When FF stops, define L = all vertices reachable from s in the residual graph, and R = everything else (including t, since t is not reachable — that's why FF stopped).

Now look at every edge crossing from L to R in the original graph:

  • Every edge u→v where u∈L, v∈R must be completely full (f(u→v) = c(u→v)). If it weren't, there'd be residual capacity forward, meaning v would be reachable from s — contradiction.
  • Every edge u→v where u∈R, v∈L (backwards edges) must carry zero flow. If it did carry flow, the backward residual edge would exist, making u reachable — contradiction.
So: size(f) = (flow on L→R edges) − (flow on R→L edges) = cap(L,R) − 0 = cap(L,R)

The flow equals a cut capacity. But we know flow ≤ any cut. So this must be the minimum cut, and the flow must be maximum. ✅

The Three Equivalent Statements

Your notes show that these three things are ALL equivalent — if any one is true, all three are true:

#StatementWhat it means
1f is a maximum flowNo valid flow is larger than f
2No augmenting path in residual graph GfFord-Fulkerson has terminated
3size(f) = cap(L,R) for some cut (L,R)Flow matches a cut = both are optimal
✨ This is why FF is correct: when it terminates (statement 2), the flow it found equals a cut capacity (statement 3), which means it's the maximum possible flow (statement 1). All three are guaranteed simultaneously.

Chapter 6 / 6 Wrap-Up

The Full Story in One Picture 🗺️

Here's how everything from lectures 2 and 3 connects:

Network → you want to maximize flow from s to t
↓
Ford-Fulkerson → keep finding augmenting paths in the residual graph
↓
Terminates → no path from s to t in residual graph
↓
At termination → reachable vertices = L, rest = R → this IS the min cut
↓
Max-Flow = Min-Cut → the flow found is provably optimal
