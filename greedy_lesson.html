The Setup: Study Scheduling

There are n psychology studies. Each study i has:

  • A payout p_i ≥ 0 dollars
  • A deadline d_i ≥ 0 — the last day to participate

Today is day 0. Lucky can do one study per day and can choose any day up to the deadline. He wants to maximize total payout.

🎯

This is the classic Job Scheduling with Deadlines problem. It appears in many forms — task scheduling, exam prep, project deadlines. The greedy insight is powerful once you see it.

Problem 3.1
8 pts — Counterexample

Why Lucky's Greedy Fails

The Question Lucky's strategy: every day, pick the remaining study with the highest payout, and do it that day (if its deadline hasn't passed). Prove by counterexample that this does NOT always maximize payout.

Lucky's algorithm processes studies greedily by payout — always picking the most valuable remaining study and scheduling it at the earliest available day. The flaw is subtle: greedily grabbing the best thing now can permanently block you from doing a cluster of moderately-valuable things later.

🔍 The Core Intuition

Scheduling at the first available day is wasteful. It wastes an early slot on a study that could have been done later, leaving you unable to do a study with a tight early deadline.

Think of it like this: if you have a task due Monday (only day available) and another due Friday, doing the Friday task on Monday wastes Monday. Now you can't do the Monday-due task at all.

📝 What Makes a Good Counterexample?

For a counterexample proof, you need to show:

  1. A specific instance (list of studies with deadlines and payouts)
  2. What Lucky's algorithm does on that instance
  3. A better schedule that earns more money

You don't need to prove that the better schedule is optimal — you just need to show Lucky's schedule isn't.

Lucky greedily grabs the highest-payout study A on day 0, but A doesn't need day 0 — it could have been done on day 1. By using day 0 for A, Lucky loses the chance to do B on day 0.

Problem 3.2
25 pts — Formal Proof of Correctness

Plucky's Fix — Scheduling as Late as Possible

The Question Plucky's strategy: process studies in decreasing payout order, but schedule each study on the last available day ≤ its deadline. Prove this always returns the maximum payout.

🔑 The Key Insight

Scheduling as late as possible is the fix. By pushing every study to its latest possible slot, we preserve earlier days for studies with earlier deadlines — the studies that have no flexibility. This is sometimes called "don't waste early slots."

Think of it like airline boarding — put the bulky bags in the back to keep the front clear for tight connections.

The Exchange Argument Proof

💡

Exchange arguments are the standard technique for proving greedy algorithms correct. The idea: take any optimal solution, and show you can convert it into Plucky's solution one swap at a time without losing any value. Therefore Plucky's solution must also be optimal.

▶ Step 1: Claim what Plucky's algorithm outputs

Plucky's algorithm produces a schedule S_P — a set of (study, day) assignments. We claim S_P maximizes total payout.

First, note that the order Plucky processes studies (decreasing payout) doesn't affect which studies get scheduled — it affects which days they get. We need to prove that Plucky never misses a study it could have included.

▶ Step 2: Feasibility — Plucky only schedules valid assignments

Every study i that Plucky schedules is assigned to some day t ≤ d_i, because Plucky only looks at days ≤ d_i when placing study i. Each day slot is marked "booked" once used. So:

  • No two studies share the same day.
  • Every study is scheduled on or before its deadline.

Therefore Plucky's output is always a feasible schedule.

▶ Step 3: Plucky includes every study any feasible schedule can include

This is the heart of the proof. We want to show: if there exists a feasible schedule that includes study i, then Plucky's algorithm also includes study i.

Key Lemma: When Plucky processes study i, if there exists any available day t ≤ d_i, Plucky will find it and schedule study i.

Proof sketch: Plucky scans days d_i, d_i-1, ..., 0 looking for a free slot. If any such slot exists (isn't "booked"), Plucky uses it. A slot can only be "booked" if Plucky already placed a higher-payout study there. So if Plucky can't find a free slot, it means all days 0...d_i are already used by studies with higher payout — which means no feasible schedule could have preferred study i over those studies anyway.

▶ Step 4: The Exchange Argument (Core)
1

Let OPT be any optimal schedule, and let S_P be Plucky's schedule. Suppose for contradiction that OPT earns more than S_P. That means there's some study i that's in OPT but not in S_P.

2

In Plucky's schedule, since study i was not scheduled, all days 0 through d_i were already occupied when Plucky tried to place study i. Each of those occupants has payout ≥ p_i (because Plucky processes in decreasing payout order).

3

In OPT, study i is placed on some day t ≤ d_i. On that same day t, Plucky has placed some study j with p_j ≥ p_i.

4

Now swap: in OPT, replace study i (on day t) with study j. Since p_j ≥ p_i, this swap doesn't decrease total payout. We need to verify study j can legally occupy day t: by Step 3's lemma, study j had payout ≥ all studies that occupied days 0...d_j, but we need d_j ≥ t... (this requires careful case analysis in a full proof).

5

Repeating this exchange for all differences between OPT and S_P transforms OPT into S_P without decreasing payout. Therefore S_P is at least as good as OPT, which means S_P is optimal. ∎

▶ Step 5: Putting it together — full proof structure

A clean exam-ready proof has this skeleton:

// Proof of Plucky's Correctness

Claim: Plucky's algorithm returns maximum payout.

Part 1 — Feasibility:
  Plucky never assigns two studies to the same day.
  Plucky only assigns study i to day t ≤ d_i.
  ∴ Plucky's output is a valid schedule.

Part 2 — Optimality (Exchange Argument):
  Let OPT = any optimal schedule.
  Let S_P = Plucky's schedule.
  
  If OPT ≠ S_P, there's a study i ∈ OPT \ S_P.
  
  i ∉ S_P means: when Plucky tried to place i,
  all days 0..d_i were occupied by studies
  with payout ≥ p_i (processed before i).
  
  In OPT, i is on some day t ≤ d_i.
  Plucky's schedule has study j on day t, p_j ≥ p_i.
  
  We can "swap" i for j in OPT: 
    new payout ≥ old payout (since p_j ≥ p_i)
    schedule remains feasible (j fits on day t)
  
  Repeat for all differences.
  OPT is transformed to S_P without loss.
  ∴ payout(S_P) ≥ payout(OPT).
  ∴ S_P is optimal.  ∎

Problem 3.3
16 pts — Ethics in CS

Utilitarian Value — When the Algorithm Meets Ethics

📚

Context: This question uses the scheduling problem as a lens to explore utilitarianism — a major ethical framework in CS ethics. These questions have specific correct answers; they're not opinion questions. Know the framework cold.

3.3.1
4 pts

How does a utilitarian calculate value?

Utilitarianism in One Sentence

A utilitarian calculates the value of an action as the sum of wellbeing (utility/happiness) produced across all affected individuals.

Value(action) = Σ wellbeing(person_i, action) for all affected persons i

Does Lucky's Payout Count?

Yes — but only as one person's utility among many. Lucky's $10 personal payout is part of the sum, but so is:

  • The scientific value of the research
  • Benefits to future patients from the psychology findings
  • The study team's funding security
  • The participants' own experience

So Lucky's payout factors in, but it's not privileged — it's just one input.

Utilitarian Total for a Study

Lucky's payout $10
Research benefit (society) $25
Researcher utility $8
Total: $43

📝 Exam Answer Template

How a utilitarian calculates value: "A utilitarian calculates the value of each study as the total sum of wellbeing (utility) produced across all individuals affected — including researchers, future beneficiaries of the research, and society at large."

Does Lucky's payout factor in: "Yes — Lucky's personal payout does factor into the utilitarian calculation, but only as one person's share of the total utility. It is not the sole or primary measure of value; the broader societal benefits of the research carry significant weight as well."

3.3.2
4 pts

What changes to make Plucky's algorithm utilitarian?

The algorithm structure stays exactly the same — only the value used for sorting changes:

PLUCKY'S ORIGINAL
Sort studies by personal payout p_i
Maximize: Σ p_i for chosen studies
→
UTILITARIAN VERSION
Sort by total utility u_i (sum of all affected parties)
Maximize: Σ u_i for chosen studies

📝 Exam Answer Template (2–3 sentences)

"To maximize utilitarian value, Lucky should replace each study's personal payout p_i with its total utility u_i — the sum of wellbeing produced for all affected individuals including researchers, future beneficiaries of the research findings, and society. The algorithm would then sort studies in decreasing order of u_i instead of p_i. Everything else in Plucky's algorithm — the scheduling logic of placing each study at the latest available slot — remains unchanged."

3.3.3
4 pts

Why is the utilitarian calculation difficult in practice?

The Measurement Problem

Even if we agree on utilitarianism as a framework, actually computing the total utility of a study requires measuring things that are extraordinarily hard to quantify:

▶ Hard-to-measure example 1: Long-term societal benefits

A psychology study today might inform clinical treatments used 20 years from now, improving mental health outcomes for millions of people. How do you attach a dollar value (or any utility value) to that? You'd need to:

  • Estimate how many people will be affected
  • Estimate how much better off they'll be
  • Discount future benefits over time (what's $1 of benefit in 20 years worth today?)
  • Account for uncertainty — the study might not find anything useful

Each of these is itself a difficult empirical and philosophical question.

▶ Hard-to-measure example 2: Comparing utility across people

Utilitarianism requires summing utility across individuals. But utility isn't measured in dollars — it's subjective wellbeing. Is $10 of happiness to a rich person the same as $10 to a poor person? How do you compare the psychological distress of a study participant to the abstract future benefit to an unborn patient?

This is called the interpersonal comparison of utility problem, and there's no consensus solution.

▶ Hard-to-measure example 3: Scope of "affected individuals"

Who counts? Just the direct participants? The research team? All future patients who might benefit? Future generations? Non-human animals? The boundary of "affected individuals" is a philosophical choice that dramatically changes the calculation, and there's no objective way to set it.

📝 Exam Answer Template (2–3 sentences)

"The utilitarian calculation is difficult because it requires measuring quantities that are highly uncertain or subjective. For example, the long-term societal benefit of a psychology study — how many people's mental health will improve, and by how much — cannot be known with precision at the time of scheduling. Even if we could estimate these benefits, comparing utility across different individuals (e.g., equating Lucky's $10 payout with a future patient's improved quality of life) involves deep philosophical assumptions with no objective answer."

3.3.4
4 pts

Privacy violation that participants never learn about — does it affect utilitarian value?

⚖️

This is testing whether you understand that utilitarianism is strictly consequentialist — it evaluates actions based only on their outcomes (consequences), specifically changes in wellbeing. If no one's wellbeing changes, the utilitarian verdict doesn't change.

UTILITARIAN VIEW
Arguably NO effect
If participants never find out → no decrease in their wellbeing → utility sum unchanged
vs
DEONTOLOGICAL VIEW
YES — wrong regardless
Rights were violated. That's wrong whether or not anyone finds out or suffers.

⚠️ The Nuanced Utilitarian Case

A careful utilitarian might argue there are some indirect effects even if participants don't know:

  • If discovered later, it could erode trust in research broadly, reducing participation in future studies
  • The researchers' own wellbeing may be affected (guilt, institutional risk)
  • There's a risk of future harm if the data is misused
  • A rule utilitarian would argue that allowing privacy violations sets a bad precedent with negative long-run consequences

But these are second-order effects. The direct, first-order utilitarian answer is that undiscovered harms don't register in the utility calculation.

📝 Exam Answer Template (2–3 sentences)

"Under strict utilitarianism, the privacy violation would not affect the study's value if participants never discover it — utilitarianism evaluates actions purely by their consequences on wellbeing, and an undiscovered violation produces no decrease in participants' experienced wellbeing. However, a more careful utilitarian analysis might note indirect effects: if the violation were ever discovered, it could erode public trust in research institutions, reducing participation in future studies and lowering overall societal utility. A rule utilitarian would additionally argue that permitting privacy violations even when undetected encourages a culture with harmful long-run consequences."
