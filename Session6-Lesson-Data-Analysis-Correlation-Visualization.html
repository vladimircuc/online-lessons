Data Analysis, Correlation & Visualization

How to extract information from data, what correlation actually means (and doesn't mean), how visualizations can inform or mislead, and how to handle the reading passage questions on the exam. This finishes Big Idea 2.

In this lesson: 1. Extracting Information from Data 2. Cleaning & Combining Data 3. Correlation vs Causation 4. Data Visualization & Misleading Graphs 5. Reading Passage Questions (Exam Strategy) 6. End of Lesson Review (18 Questions)

1. Extracting Information from Data

Data is raw values (numbers, text, dates). Information is what you get when you process, analyze, or interpret that data. The exam tests whether you understand the difference.

Data → Processing → Information

Data:       [85, 92, 78, 95, 88, 71, 90]
             (just numbers, no meaning by themselves)

Processing: Compute the average
             sum = 599, count = 7, avg = 85.6

Information: "The class average is 85.6"
             (now it MEANS something)

What You Can Extract

From a dataset, you can compute:
  - Average (mean)
  - Minimum / Maximum
  - Count (how many match a condition)
  - Trends over time (increasing, decreasing, stable)
  - Patterns (which category is most common?)
  - Comparisons between groups

You CANNOT determine from data alone:
  - WHY something happened (causation)
  - What WILL happen in the future (prediction needs a model)
  - Whether the data collection was fair/unbiased

Large Data Sets

The exam specifically mentions working with large data sets. Key points:

Benefits of large datasets:
  - More data → more reliable patterns
  - Can reveal trends invisible in small samples
  - Can be processed by computers (humans can't manually)

Challenges:
  - May contain errors, missing values, duplicates
  - Storage and processing require significant resources
  - Privacy concerns when data contains PII
  - Bias in HOW data was collected carries into results
Exam pattern: "What information can be determined from this data?" questions. The answer is always something you can COMPUTE from the values. If the answer requires knowing WHY or making assumptions not in the data, it's wrong.

Quick Check: Extracting Information

A dataset contains the daily high temperature for a city over 365 days. Which CAN be determined from this data? A. The hottest day of the year B. Why July was the hottest month C. Whether climate change caused the heat D. What the temperature will be next year
A. The hottest day of the year. This is a simple max operation on the data. B requires knowing WHY (causation). C requires scientific analysis beyond this data. D requires prediction, not just this year's data. The exam always asks: "can you COMPUTE this from the given data, or does it require outside information?"
A school collects: student name, grade level, GPA, and number of absences for all students. Name two pieces of information that can be extracted from this data.
Examples: 1. "The average GPA for 10th graders is 3.2" (filter by grade, compute average) 2. "Students with fewer than 5 absences have a higher average GPA than those with 10+" (filter into groups, compare averages) Other valid answers: - Which grade level has the highest average GPA - The student with the most absences - The correlation between absences and GPA

2. Cleaning & Combining Data

Data Cleaning

Real-world data is messy. Before analyzing, you often need to clean it:

Common problems:
  - Missing values (blank cells, null entries)
  - Duplicates (same record appears twice)
  - Inconsistent formatting ("USA", "US", "United States")
  - Errors/typos (age = 999, temperature = -500°F)
  - Outdated information

Cleaning steps:
  - Remove or fill in missing values
  - Remove duplicates
  - Standardize formats
  - Fix or remove obvious errors
  - Validate against known constraints

Combining Data from Multiple Sources

Merging datasets can reveal insights that neither dataset alone could show.

Example:
  Dataset A: Student names and test scores
  Dataset B: Student names and hours of sleep

  Combined: Can now explore the RELATIONSHIP
  between sleep and test performance.

Challenges of combining:
  - Different formats (dates, names, units)
  - Missing matches (student in A but not in B)
  - Different collection methods → different biases
  - Privacy: combining datasets can re-identify
    people even if individual sets are anonymous
Re-identification risk. Two anonymous datasets, when combined, can sometimes identify individuals. Example: a dataset of "zip code, age, gender" plus a dataset of "zip code, age, medical condition" could reveal who has which condition. The exam tests this privacy concept.

Quick Check: Data Cleaning

A survey dataset has these issues: - 15 out of 1000 responses have blank ages - Some entries list "New York" and others "NY" - One entry has age listed as 350 What cleaning steps are needed?
1. Handle missing ages: either remove those 15 rows or fill in with a reasonable estimate 2. Standardize location names: convert all "New York" to "NY" (or vice versa) so they group together in analysis 3. Fix or remove the age=350 entry: this is clearly an error (no human is 350 years old) All three must be done BEFORE analysis, otherwise results will be inaccurate.

3. Correlation vs Causation

This is one of the most tested concepts on the AP CSP exam. You WILL see at least 1-2 questions on this.

Correlation: Two Things Happen Together

Correlation means two variables tend to
change together in a pattern.

Positive correlation:
  As one goes up, the other goes up too.
  Example: Height and shoe size.
  Taller people tend to have bigger feet.

Negative correlation:
  As one goes up, the other goes down.
  Example: Temperature and heating bill.
  Warmer weather → lower heating costs.

No correlation:
  No pattern between the two variables.
  Example: Shoe size and test scores.

Causation: One Thing CAUSES the Other

Causation means one variable DIRECTLY
causes the other to change.

This is MUCH harder to prove than correlation.

Just because two things correlate does NOT
mean one causes the other.

The Classic Examples

Example 1: Ice cream sales and drownings
  Both increase in summer.
  Correlation? YES.
  Does ice cream CAUSE drowning? NO.
  Hidden variable: HOT WEATHER causes both.

Example 2: Shoe size and reading level in kids
  Kids with bigger feet read better.
  Correlation? YES.
  Do big feet CAUSE better reading? NO.
  Hidden variable: AGE. Older kids have
  bigger feet AND read better.

Example 3: Smoking and lung cancer
  Smokers get lung cancer more often.
  Correlation? YES.
  Does smoking CAUSE lung cancer? YES.
  (Proven through decades of controlled studies,
  not just observational data.)
The exam's favorite trap: giving you a correlation and asking which conclusion is valid. The WRONG answers will claim causation. The RIGHT answer will say something like "there is an association between X and Y" or "X and Y are correlated." If an answer says "X causes Y" based on data alone, it's almost always wrong.

Confounding Variables (Lurking Variables)

A confounding variable is a hidden third factor that influences both variables, creating a false appearance of causation.

The pattern on the exam:
  "Data shows A and B are correlated.
   Which BEST explains this relationship?"

  Wrong answer: "A causes B"
  Wrong answer: "B causes A"
  Right answer:  "A third variable C could
                  influence both A and B"
  OR
  Right answer:  "There is a correlation but
                  causation cannot be determined"

Quick Check: Correlation vs Causation

A study finds that students who eat breakfast score higher on exams. Which conclusion is VALID?
VALID: "There is a positive correlation between eating breakfast and exam scores." NOT VALID: "Eating breakfast causes higher exam scores." Possible confounding variables: - Students who eat breakfast may have more stable home lives (better study habits) - They may be morning people (more alert) - Wealthier families → both better nutrition AND better educational resources Correlation ≠ causation!
Data shows cities with more police officers also have higher crime rates. Does hiring police cause crime?
No. The causation likely runs the OTHER way (or through a confounding variable): - Cities with HIGH CRIME hire more police (crime causes more hiring, not the reverse) - Larger cities have both more crime AND more police (population is the confounding variable) This is a classic example of REVERSE CAUSATION and confounding variables. The data shows correlation, but the obvious causal conclusion is backwards.

4. Data Visualization & Misleading Graphs

Why Visualize?

Benefits:
  - Patterns visible at a glance
  - Easier to communicate findings
  - Can reveal trends, outliers, clusters
  - Accessible to non-technical audiences

Choosing the right chart:
  Bar chart:  Compare categories
  Line graph: Show change over time (trends)
  Pie chart:  Show parts of a whole (proportions)
  Scatter plot: Show relationship between 2 variables
  Histogram:  Show distribution of values

How Graphs Can MISLEAD

This is heavily tested. The exam will show you a graph and ask what's wrong with it.

Trick 1: Truncated Y-axis
  Starting the y-axis at 95 instead of 0
  makes a difference of 95→100 look HUGE.
  A 5% increase looks like a 100% increase.

Trick 2: Cherry-picked time range
  Showing only Jan-Mar of a stock that
  happened to go up, ignoring the rest
  of the year when it crashed.

Trick 3: Manipulated scale
  Unequal intervals on the x or y axis.
  Stretching or compressing the scale
  to exaggerate or minimize changes.

Trick 4: Missing context
  "Sales doubled!" (from 2 to 4)
  Sounds impressive without knowing
  the competitor sold 10,000.

Trick 5: Correlation presented as causation
  Graph title says "X causes Y" when
  the data only shows correlation.

Trick 6: Biased sample
  Survey of only your friends about
  "best pizza place" isn't representative.
The exam question pattern: "Which of the following could be a problem with the conclusion drawn from this visualization?" Look for truncated axes, cherry-picked data, small sample sizes, or causal claims from correlational data.

Quick Check: Visualization

A company's graph shows profit "soaring" from $1,000,000 to $1,050,000. The y-axis starts at $990,000. What is misleading about this graph?
The y-axis is TRUNCATED. By starting at $990,000 instead of $0, the $50,000 increase (5%) looks enormous visually, taking up most of the chart height. If the y-axis started at $0, the increase would look like a tiny bump, which is more accurate for a 5% change. This is the #1 misleading graph trick on the AP exam.
A news article shows that a city's crime rate dropped 50% from 2023 to 2024. The data shows: 4 crimes in 2023, 2 crimes in 2024. What's the problem?
The sample size is too small. "50% drop" is technically true (4 → 2), but the numbers are so tiny that random variation could easily explain the change. One more crime in 2024 would make it a 25% drop instead of 50%. Small numbers make percentages misleading. Always look at the RAW numbers, not just the percentages.

5. Reading Passage Questions (Exam Strategy)

5 of the 70 MCQ come with a reading passage about a computing innovation. These aren't harder, just longer. Here's how to handle them.

What These Look Like

You'll get a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) describing a computing innovation, like a fitness tracker, a recommendation algorithm, or a smart home system. Then 1-2 questions about it.

Strategy

1. Read the passage FIRST, not the questions.
   Understand what the innovation does before
   looking at what they're asking.

2. Identify the data.
   What data does it collect? How is it used?
   Is there PII? What metadata might exist?

3. Think about impact.
   What's the benefit? What's the concern?
   Who benefits? Who might be harmed?

4. Watch for correlation/causation traps.
   If the passage says "users who do X also
   do Y," that's correlation, not causation.

5. Eliminate obviously wrong answers.
   Usually 2 answers are clearly wrong,
   leaving you to decide between 2 plausible ones.

Practice Passage

A fitness app collects users' daily step counts, heart rate, sleep duration, and GPS location data. The app uses this data to provide personalized health recommendations and displays trends over time through graphs and charts. Users can share their data with friends through a social feature. The company that makes the app states in their privacy policy that anonymized user data may be shared with third-party health researchers.

Practice: Passage Questions

Based on the passage above: Which of the following is a privacy concern with this app?
GPS location data is PII and could reveal where users live, work, and travel. Even "anonymized" data could potentially be re-identified when combined with other datasets (re-identification risk). The social sharing feature could expose health data to unintended audiences. Third-party sharing means users lose control over how their data is used.
The app shows a graph indicating that users who walk 10,000+ steps per day have lower resting heart rates. Which conclusion is supported by this data?
SUPPORTED: "There is a correlation between higher step counts and lower resting heart rate." NOT SUPPORTED: "Walking 10,000 steps per day causes a lower resting heart rate." Why not causation? People who walk more may also eat healthier, be younger, or have fewer health conditions. Without a controlled study, causation cannot be established from app data.
